# Coaching Questions — Market Opportunity

Extended question bank and exercise guides for the `canvas-opportunity` skill.

---

## Box A: Customer/User Insight — Extended Questions

### Demographic + Situational
- How old are they?
- What's their job title or role? At what size company?
- Are they a decision-maker, influencer, or end user (or some combination)?
- What tools do they use today to manage the problem your product solves?
- How do they currently solve this problem — or do they?

### Psychographic
- What do they read? Who do they follow? Where do they spend time online?
- What does success look like for them professionally in the next 12 months?
- What keeps them up at night that your product could address?
- If they were describing their problem to a peer, what words would they use?
- What are they embarrassed to admit about the problem they have?

### Behavioral
- How did they first recognize the problem your product solves?
- What did they try before finding (or considering) you?
- What would make them switch away from you to a competitor?
- What does a great day using your product look like vs. a frustrating one?

## Anti-Audience Exercise (Ries & Trout)

The anti-audience is not "people who won't buy." It's "people you consciously choose not to serve" — and making that choice sharpens who you *are* for.

### Questions to Define the Anti-Audience
1. Who would be frustrated by your product even if it worked perfectly?
2. Who has needs that, if you tried to address them, would force you to compromise for your primary customer?
3. Who would misuse your product or represent you badly as a customer?
4. Who is adjacent to your audience but distinctly different?

### Why It Matters
Every dollar spent trying to appeal to the anti-audience is a dollar taken from the primary customer. More importantly: a brand that tries to be for everyone signals that it's for no one.

### Examples
- **Mailchimp** didn't optimize for enterprise marketing departments with large teams and dedicated ops. Their anti-audience was large businesses that needed customization, complex integrations, and dedicated support. That freed them to optimize for small businesses who needed ease and self-serve.
- **Basecamp** explicitly chose not to serve organizations that needed traditional project management workflows (Gantt charts, resource management). This wasn't a limitation — it was a choice that clarified their positioning.

## Multiple Audiences Decision Tree

**When to create multiple canvases:**
1. Do you serve two meaningfully different audiences? (E.g., buyers vs. users, marketplace sides, B2B2C)
2. Would the positioning statement you'd write for each audience be genuinely different?
3. Would the emotional benefits for each audience be different?

If yes to any of these: separate canvases.

**Common patterns that need multiple canvases:**
- **Marketplaces:** Riders and drivers (Uber), buyers and sellers (Etsy), hosts and guests (Airbnb)
- **B2B2C:** The IT buyer vs. the daily user are often different people with different priorities
- **Freemium:** Free tier users often have different profiles and needs than paid tier users
- **Developer tools:** Developer who evaluates vs. engineer who uses daily vs. business buyer who pays

**Which canvas to build first:** Start with the audience who drives the purchase decision or who is most critical to achieving product-market fit.

## Box B: Competitive Environment — Extended Questions

### Competitive Mapping
- What are the 5 most common searches your customer does before finding you?
- What shows up? What language does the category use?
- Who are the category leaders? What do they say about themselves?
- What are the category challengers doing differently?

### White Space Analysis
- What emotional territory does no competitor own?
- What problem does the category acknowledge but not solve?
- What does the category *not* say — the unspoken assumption everyone shares?
- What would the anti-consensus position in your category be?

### Mental Model Mapping
- What metaphor or frame does your customer use to think about your category?
- Is that frame helpful or limiting for you?
- If you could shift their mental model, what would you shift it *to*?

## Box C: Features — Extended Questions

### Feature Prioritization
- Which features do customers use first?
- Which features cause the "aha moment" — the moment they understand the product's value?
- Which features appear in every sales call? Which are rarely mentioned?
- Which features are already table-stakes in the category?
- Which features would cause customers to switch away if you removed them?

### Defensibility Assessment
- Is this feature protected by IP, data, network effects, or switching costs?
- How long would it take a well-funded competitor to replicate it?
- Is it hard to replicate because of technology, or because of unique knowledge or relationships?

### Feature to Benefit Bridge
After listing features: "For each of these, what does a customer actually *do* differently because of this feature? How does it change their day or their work?"
