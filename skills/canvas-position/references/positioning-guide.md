# Positioning Guide

Reference guide for the `canvas-position` skill.

---

## Template Anatomy

```
For [audience (A)], at a time when [cultural moment (B)],
[brand] is the [description] that [benefit]
because [proof (D)] so that [payoff (E)].
```

### Breaking Down Each Component

**"For [audience (A)]"**
This is the qualifier. Who is this brand *specifically* for? If you've done Box A well, this should be almost verbatim from your customer portrait. Don't soften it with vague segmentation. "For small businesses" is too broad. "For growing businesses with strong opinions" (Mailchimp) is specific.

The audience clause immediately tells the anti-audience: *this isn't for you.* That's a feature.

**"at a time when [cultural moment (B)]"**
The timing clause. What's shifting in culture, technology, or the market that makes this brand timely right now? Drawn from Box B. This clause is the most dynamic element of the positioning statement — it may need to be updated as the moment evolves, even while the rest of the statement remains stable.

A good cultural moment clause makes the brand feel prescient rather than reactive. It names something the audience is already feeling but hasn't necessarily articulated. Examples:
- "at a time when agentic coding is everywhere but nobody has solved the context problem"
- "at a time when small businesses are moving online but marketing tools are still built for enterprise teams"
- "at a time when every team runs on data but most BI tools require a data scientist to operate"

**"[brand] is the [description] that"**
The category and differentiator. What type of thing is your brand? What category does it live in? What's the one-word or short-phrase modifier that sets it apart from others in that category?

Examples:
- "is the *creative* marketing platform" (not just a marketing platform)
- "is the *human* CRM" (not just a CRM)
- "is the *self-serve* analytics tool" (not just analytics)

**"[benefit]"**
The main value proposition — drawn from your rational and emotional benefits. Not what the product does; what the customer *gets*. The highest-credibility benefit that also resonates emotionally.

**"because [proof (D)]"**
The reason to believe. Drawn from Box D (Features). Why should the audience believe the benefit is true? What evidence does the product offer? Keep it specific — "because of our comprehensive platform that anyone can use" is better than "because of our advanced technology."

**"so that [payoff (E)]"**
The emotional payoff. The "so that" moves the statement to the highest level of abstraction — what the brand ultimately gives the customer at a human level. Drawn from your Emotional Benefits. Aspirational but not hollow.

## 5 Worked Examples

### 1. Mailchimp (primary example from the book)
**Statement:** For growing businesses with strong opinions, at a time when small businesses were moving online but professional marketing tools were still built for enterprise teams, MailChimp is the creative marketing platform that helps you find your people and keep them engaged because of our comprehensive platform that anyone can use, so that you can be yourself on a bigger stage.

**Brand Essence:** Keep growing.

**What works:** "Strong opinions" identifies and includes the audience. The cultural moment clause names the exact structural gap Mailchimp was built to fill — the tools hadn't caught up to the democratization of the audience. "Creative" positions against enterprise tools that feel corporate. "Be yourself on a bigger stage" is earned through the rational benefit of an accessible platform.

---

### 2. Basecamp (project management)
**Statement:** For small, independent companies who believe in doing less better, at a time when the default of software teams had become more meetings, more tools, and more noise, Basecamp is the calm, focused project management tool that replaces the noise of email, Slack, and spreadsheets because everything your team needs is in one organized place, so that you can do your best work without being dragged into meetings and messages all day.

**Brand Essence:** Work calm.

**What works:** "Small, independent companies who believe in doing less better" is extremely specific — includes the right people, excludes enterprise customers. The cultural moment names the tool proliferation problem without requiring the audience to learn new language. "Calm" is a genuine differentiator in a noisy category.

---

### 3. Notion (workspace tool)
**Statement:** For knowledge workers who want to think, not just organize, at a time when every team had accumulated five different tools to do what one connected workspace could do, Notion is the flexible workspace that connects your notes, docs, and databases the way your mind connects ideas because you can build any system you can imagine, so that your tools finally work the way you do.

**Brand Essence:** Build your way.

---

### 4. Stripe (payment infrastructure)
**Statement:** For developer-first companies who need payments to work reliably and stay out of the way, at a time when internet commerce was exploding but payment integrations still required weeks of engineering time, Stripe is the payment infrastructure that turns a week-long integration into an afternoon because of the most comprehensive and well-documented API in the industry, so that you can ship and iterate without payments becoming a ceiling on your ambition.

**Brand Essence:** Commerce for the internet.

---

### 5. Linear (issue tracking)
**Statement:** For software teams who take quality seriously, at a time when issue trackers had become so bloated with process that the tool itself had become the work, Linear is the streamlined project tool that makes the work of building software feel as good as the software you build because of an opinionated, fast-by-default system that removes friction from every workflow, so that you can focus on the work that actually matters.

**Brand Essence:** Purpose-built for builders.

## Category Creation vs. Category Occupation

Most positioning is about *occupying* a position in an existing category with a clear modifier. But occasionally, a company is creating a new category.

**Category occupation (most common):**
"We're a [modifier] [existing category]"
- "The creative marketing platform" (marketing platform = existing category)
- "The calm project management tool" (PM tool = existing category)

This is easier. The audience already has a frame for what you do — you're claiming a specific position within that frame.

**Category creation (rarer, more expensive):**
"We're inventing a new type of thing"
- "Customer success platform" (Gainsight — had to define a whole new category)
- "Revenue operations" (had to educate the market on what this even meant)

Category creation requires significant marketing investment to build the category itself before you can own a position in it. Only worth it if no existing category captures what you do. When in doubt: occupy first.

**Diagnostic question:** "If I said one sentence describing my product to my ideal customer, would they immediately understand what type of thing it is? Or would they say 'huh — what is that exactly?'"

## Common Positioning Mistakes

### 1. The Generic Claim
"We help [companies/people] [do something] [better/faster/smarter]."
This could describe literally anything.

### 2. The Feature Dump
"For [audience], [brand] is the all-in-one platform that [lists 7 features]..."
A list of features is not a positioning statement. One core benefit, earned through proof.

### 3. The Aspirational Lie
Making a claim that isn't true yet — or that you can't substantiate. Positioning statements can be aspirational, but they must be achievable within 12–18 months or they break trust.

### 4. The Me-Too Statement
A statement that, if you replaced your brand name with a competitor's name, still works. If your top competitor could use your statement word-for-word, it's not differentiated enough.

### 5. The Insider Language Statement
Language that resonates internally but means nothing to your customer. "The paradigm-shifting collaborative intelligence layer" — who is that for?

## Brand Essence Patterns

The Brand Essence is 2–4 words that crystallize the positioning statement. Not a tagline (though it can become one). An internal compass.

**Action + object:**
"Keep growing" — Mailchimp
"Just do it" — Nike
"Think different" — Apple

**For + identity:**
"For the bold"
"For the self-serious builder"
"For the ones who ship"

**Noun phrase (a state or quality):**
"Work calm"
"Clarity at scale"
"Speed with purpose"

**Belief statement:**
"Commerce belongs to the makers"
"Simple wins"

**What Brand Essence is NOT:**
- A mission statement
- A tagline (though it might become one)
- A description of product features
- A value that could describe any good company

**Test:** Does the Brand Essence orient a decision? If you're debating two directions in a campaign, would it help you choose? If not, it's not sharp enough.
