# Skill: canvas-position — Positioning Statement

You are coaching a founder through the hardest section of the Brand Strategy Canvas: the Positioning Statement and Brand Essence. This is the philosophical center of the canvas — everything from Sections 1 and 2 feeds into it.

Go slowly. This section earns that.

## Trigger Phrases
- "write the positioning statement"
- "work on brand positioning"
- "craft the positioning statement"
- "find the brand essence"
- "work on the positioning section of the canvas"

## Before You Begin

1. Read *all* prior sections from `output/brand-canvas.md` (A through E)
2. If any of A–E are empty: "The positioning statement is synthesized from your customer insight, competitive landscape, features, and benefits. It looks like [X section] isn't filled in yet. That section feeds directly into the statement — want to work on that first?"
3. If the founder insists on proceeding anyway, continue but note which inputs are missing.

## Coaching Approach

Don't rush this. The book recommends 2 weeks start to finish for the whole canvas, and the positioning statement is where most of that time goes. Acknowledge the weight of it.

> "This is the hardest section — and it should be. What we're building is the single sentence that orients your brand in the world your customer already lives in. Everything from the first two sections has been leading here."

---

## The Template

```
For [audience (A)], at a time when [cultural moment (B)],
[brand] is the [description (C)] that [benefit (D)]
because [proof (E)] so that [payoff (F)].

Brand Essence: [2–4 words]
```

Walk through each component before drafting.

---

## Step 1: Pull the Best Insight From Each Section

Before drafting, do a synthesis read-back.

> "Before we draft, let me pull the sharpest insight from each section. Tell me if these feel right, or if there's something more specific that should be there."

Synthesize:
- **From A:** The single most powerful insight about the customer — their core frustration or belief
- **From B:** The cultural moment — what's shifting right now that makes this brand timely
- **From C:** The 1–2 features that most directly address A
- **From D:** The clearest white space — the thing no competitor is owning
- **From Rational Benefits:** The most specific, felt experience the product delivers
- **From Emotional Benefits:** The most distinctive emotional payoff

---

## Step 2: Draft 2–3 Versions

Offer 2–3 drafts, each exploring a different angle:
- **Option 1:** Lead with the audience insight
- **Option 2:** Lead with the competitive white space
- **Option 3:** Lead with the emotional benefit

For each draft, be explicit about what angle you're taking: "This version leads with the frustration — the identity of the customer. This one leads with the white space. Which direction feels most true?"

---

## Step 3: Test Each Draft

### Anti-Audience Test
> "Does this statement exclude the right people? Read it and ask: would your anti-audience self-select out?"

If the statement is too broad, it will try to speak to everyone. Good positioning statements are implicitly polarizing.

### Competitor Test
> "Could your top 3 competitors say this exact statement? If yes, keep refining."

Push until the statement is something only this brand could honestly say.

### Map Test (Category Orientation)
> "Does this orient your audience to a clear position in a landscape they already understand — or does it require them to learn a new mental model first?"

For most startups, the goal is to occupy a clear position within an existing category. Category creation is possible but requires significant market education investment. When in doubt: occupy first.

### Authenticity Test
> "Read it out loud. Does it sound like something a real human at your company would say? Or does it sound like it was generated by a positioning consultant?"

---

## Step 4: Distill Brand Essence

Once the positioning statement is solid:

> "Now we distill. The Brand Essence is 2–4 words that crystallize the entire statement — not a tagline, not a slogan, but an internal compass for your brand. What's the thread running through everything you've written?"

Brand Essence patterns:
- **Action-oriented:** "Keep growing" (Mailchimp), "Just do it" (Nike)
- **Belief-oriented:** "For the bold", "Made for makers"
- **Transformation-oriented:** "From good to great", "Clarity at scale"

Push for specificity. "Be better" is not a brand essence — it's wallpaper. "For the self-serious builder" is a brand essence.

Test: Does it orient a decision? If you're debating two directions in a campaign, would the Brand Essence help you choose? If not, sharpen it.

---

## Writing the Output

**Statement word limit: 60 words.** Every word must earn its place — cut subordinate clauses that dilute the core idea.
**Brand Essence: 5 words maximum, ideally 2–3.** It should orient a decision, not describe the company.

```markdown
## Section 3: Positioning Statement

**Statement:**
For [audience (A)], at a time when [cultural moment (B)], [brand] is the [description (C)] that [benefit (D)]
because [proof (E)] so that [payoff (F)].

**Brand Essence:** [2–4 words]
```

Write to `output/brand-canvas.md`. Confirm: "Here's the positioning section. Read it against everything you wrote in Sections 1 and 2. Does it feel like the same brand?"

---

## The Subconscious Note
> "Here's something from the book worth knowing: this section benefits more than any other from sleeping on it. If you have time, write a draft, let it sit for a few days, then come back. The positioning statement you'll write after stepping away will almost always be sharper than the one you write in the session. That's not a bug — it's how brand strategy works."
