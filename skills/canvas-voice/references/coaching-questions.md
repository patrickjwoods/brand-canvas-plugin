## Values Clustering Exercise

After the brainstorm:
1. Print or list all words on a surface
2. Group words that feel related — don't force them into categories you decide in advance, let the clusters emerge
3. For each cluster, find the single word that best represents it (not the most generic one — the most *specific* one)
4. Rank the clusters: which is most important to who you are today? Which is most distinctively *you*?

**Example clustering:**
- Cluster 1: "Curiosity, learning, experimentation, openness" → **Curiosity**
- Cluster 2: "Directness, honesty, transparency" → **Directness** (more specific than "honesty")
- Cluster 3: "Grit, resilience, dedication" → **Grit**

## Values Refinement Questions

For each candidate value:
1. "Would you hire someone specifically because they embody this value — even if they were weaker in other areas?"
2. "Would you fire someone, or part ways with a customer, specifically because they violated this value?"
3. "Is this value distinctive to your company — or is it table stakes for any good company?"
4. "How do you currently demonstrate this value in your product, your hiring, or your operations?"

If a value fails question 3, challenge: "Every company would say they have 'integrity.' What word would be more specific to *how* you work?"

## Personality: Bar Exercise

The bar exercise forces concrete, imaginative answers rather than abstract adjectives.

### Full Prompt
> "Let's imagine your brand as a person — not a spokesperson, just a person. Think about:
> - What do they order at a bar? (Or coffee shop — what's their usual?)
> - Where do they spend a Saturday afternoon?
> - What are they reading right now?
> - What's the last thing they'd do that would genuinely surprise you?
> - What would they argue about at dinner?"

### What to Listen For
- **Bar order:** Whiskey neat = direct, unpretentious, confident. Craft cocktail = aspirational, detail-oriented, trend-aware. Beer = approachable, laid-back. Wine = sophisticated, considered.
- **Saturday:** Hiking = health-oriented, independent, adventurous. Museum = curious, intellectual, cultured. Home cooking for friends = warm, grounded, communal.
- **Reading:** Fiction vs. non-fiction, genre, authors — signals intellectual style
- **Arguing about:** What does this person care about enough to push back on?

Use the answers to write 2–3 sentences describing the brand's personality, then extract adjectives.

## Personality: Strong Opinions Exercise

### Prompt
> "What would your brand say about the world that some people would disagree with? What position would it take that isn't safe?"

### Why This Matters
A brand without opinions disappears. Having a clear worldview attracts the right audience with much more force. The customers it repels are often the wrong ones anyway.

### Starter Questions
1. "What's wrong with how your industry has been doing things?"
2. "What does your brand believe about how work should be done — even if other companies disagree?"
3. "What does your brand believe about how customers or users should be treated — even if that's unusual?"
4. "What common practice in your category would your brand refuse to do on principle?"

### Example Opinions
- "Most marketing is condescending. We don't treat our audience like they need to be convinced — we treat them like they already know what they want."
- "Simplicity isn't a design choice. It's a business choice. We'd rather remove a feature than add one that doesn't earn its presence."
- "We believe founders, not VCs, should set the pace of growth."

## Personality: Spectrums

Place the brand on each spectrum. Be honest — many companies try to land in the middle of every spectrum, which just means they have no personality.

**Formal ↔ Casual**
Formal: precise language, professional tone, third-person distance, no contractions
Casual: conversational, first-person, colloquial, contractions, humor

**Serious ↔ Playful**
Serious: every communication has weight, rarely jokes, treats subject with gravity
Playful: comfortable with wit, uses humor as a device, doesn't take itself too seriously

**Established ↔ Challenger**
Established: references tradition, speaks with authority, plays from strength
Challenger: questions the status quo, speaks from conviction, plays from ambition

**Minimalist ↔ Expressive**
Minimalist: restraint, white space, economy of words
Expressive: abundance, personality in every corner, distinctive voice

## Key Messages: Format and Anti-Patterns

### The Message Map Format
```
Message 1: [Big Idea — a reusable concept, not a headline]
Description: [1–2 sentences expanding on the concept]
Proof points:
  - [Specific, credible proof — a stat, a story, a feature, an outcome]
  - [...]
  - [...]
Type: Inarguably true / Aspirational

Message 2: ...
```

### Talk Like a Human Test — Failure Patterns
If a message contains any of these, rewrite it:
- "We empower [audience] to [verb their way to success]" — hollow
- "A comprehensive/all-in-one solution for..." — imprecise
- "Leveraging [technology] to [transform/disrupt/revolutionize]..." — cliché
- "Best-in-class [anything]" — meaningless without comparison
- "Seamless experience" — everyone says this
- "World-class [team/technology/support]" — filler
- "At the intersection of [X] and [Y]" — overused framing

The test: say it out loud. Would you actually say that to a real person? If you'd feel embarrassed saying it at a dinner party — rewrite it.

### Two Types of Messages — Examples

**Inarguably true (right now):**
"Mailchimp is the most widely used email marketing platform among small businesses — used by over 12 million customers."

This is provable today. Category leadership backed by data.

**Aspirational (where you're going):**
"Mailchimp is becoming the operating system for small business marketing."

This is directional — it describes the vision of what the platform is evolving toward, not a claim you can prove today. Aspirational messages orient the audience toward what you're building.

Using both types gives you messages for today's sales conversations AND tomorrow's category positioning.
